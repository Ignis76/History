Heirs of the Ulug Ulus

The Fragmentation of the Golden Horde & The Rise of the Kazakh Khanate

Seminar III • Historical Study

The mid-15th century witnessed the shattering of the Golden Horde (Ulug Ulus). From its fragments emerged new political formations. This seminar analyzes how the Kazakh Khanate arose not merely from rebellion, but as a restoration of Jochid legitimacy in the Eastern Steppe.

Structural Fragmentation

Vasary argues that the collapse was a long-term structural process. The "Ulug Ulus" decentralized into smaller Khanates (Kazan, Crimea, Astrakhan).

The rise of the Kazakhs was a geopolitical necessity caused by the power vacuum in the Eastern Desht-i Kipchak and Abulkhair Khan's inability to defend against the Oirats.

II. Evolution of Identity

How a social condition became a nation.

1. Social Condition

The term "qazaq" originally meant "vagabond," "free adventurer," or "one who separates from his tribe." It was not an ethnicity, but a status of political independence.

2. Political Allegiance (1460s)

When the tribes followed Kerey and Janibek, they were collectively called "Uzbek-Qazaqs" (The dissident Uzbeks). The term signified their political break from Abulkhair.

3. Ethnic Identifier (1500s+)

As the state consolidated and the rival "Uzbeks" moved south to Transoxiana, the "Uzbek" prefix was dropped. The people of the steppe became simply Qazaqs.

IV. Structure: The Three Zhuz

Analysis by Allen J. Frank on Ecological Adaptation

Junior Zhuz (Kishi)

Geography: Western Kazakhstan.

Tribes: Alimuly, Baiuly, Zhtyru.

The "Sword" of the Khanate. Known for martial culture, bordering the Volga and Russian expansion. Managed distinct migration routes in the arid west.

Middle Zhuz (Orta)

Geography: Central & North.

Tribes: Arghyn, Naiman, Kipchak.

The "Pen" of the Khanate. The demographic core occupying the richest pasturelands. Home to famous biys (judges) and poets.

Great Zhuz (Uly)

Geography: Zhetysu (Semirechye).

Tribes: Dulat, Jalayir, Alban.

The "Tiara" of the Khanate. The ancient aristocracy controlling the Silk Road cities (Taraz). The cradle of Kazakh statehood.

V. Historical Context & State Formation

Why did the Khanate emerge in 1465? The factors beyond genealogy.

The Catalyst: Oirat Invasion (1457)

The formation of the Kazakh Khanate was directly triggered by the failure of Abulkhair Khan. In 1457, he suffered a devastating defeat against the Oirats (Jungars) near Syghanaq.


This military disgrace shattered his authority. The tribes, seeing that the Khan could no longer protect them (the primary duty of a steppe ruler), began to look for alternative leadership. This paved the way for the Hijra (migration) of Kerey and Janibek.

The Moghul Strategy

Why did Moghulistan welcome the rebels? Esen Buqa Khan of Moghulistan did not act out of charity. He controlled the Zhetysu region but was under pressure from his brother Yunus Khan and the Timurids.


By settling Kerey and Janibek in the Chu and Talas valleys, he created a strategic "buffer zone." The Kazakhs served as border guards against the expansionist Uzbeks, securing the western flank of Moghulistan.

Synthesis: From 200,000 to a Nation

Mirza Haydar notes that initially, about 200,000 people joined Kerey and Janibek. This was a classic nomadic "voting with feet"—migrating away from a bad ruler.


  • Consolidation: Under the subsequent rule of Kasym Khan (son of Janibek), the population grew to over 1 million, unifying the vast Desht-i Kipchak.
  • Total Unification: By the early 16th century, the term "Uzbek" migrated south to modern-day Uzbekistan, while the nomads who remained in the steppe adopted the identity of "Qazaq" permanently.
  • Legacy: The Khanate became the last great successor of the Golden Horde to maintain the nomadic way of life, bridging the Mongol Empire with the modern era.
